Why We Are Here

Most Groups Fail
Before They Start

Bad group meetings do not happen because people are bad at their jobs. They happen because nobody was ever taught how to communicate inside a group. People talk over each other, shut down, or disengage and the whole group suffers for it.

Today we are going to show you exactly what that looks like, break down the 5 skills that fix it, and show you the same group doing it right.

Before Training

What NOT to Do

Bad Skit Video
  • Nobody is making eye contact. Everyone is looking at their phones or laptops while someone tries to speak.
  • People keep interrupting and talking over each other. Nobody actually hears what anyone else says.
  • Arms are crossed, people are slouched, one member has their back half turned. The energy says "I do not want to be here."
  • Someone shares an idea and gets shot down immediately with no acknowledgment at all. They go quiet for the rest of the meeting.
  • Half the group is mentally elsewhere. They miss questions, give vague answers, and slow everything down.
01

Eye Contact

Are you looking at the person you are talking to?

What Goes Wrong

Looking Everywhere But the Speaker

Staring at your phone, the ceiling, or your notes while someone is talking sends a clear message: you do not care what they are saying. It shuts people down fast.

How to Build It

Look at People, Not Screens

When someone speaks, turn your body toward them and hold natural eye contact. You do not have to stare intensely. Just enough to show you are with them and tracking what they say.

Why It Matters

It Builds Trust Instantly

Eye contact is one of the fastest ways to signal respect. When group members feel seen and acknowledged, they speak more freely, share better ideas, and trust the group more.

02

Listening

Are you actually taking in what is being said?

What Goes Wrong

Waiting to Talk Instead of Listening

Most people are not listening. They are just waiting for their turn to speak. They miss the actual point, repeat things already said, and make the speaker feel completely ignored.

How to Build It

Listen First, Respond Second

Let the person finish completely before you form a response. Try repeating back what you heard in your own words before adding your thoughts. This simple habit eliminates most confusion.

Why It Matters

Better Ideas Come Through

When people feel genuinely heard, they go deeper with their ideas. Groups that listen well surface better solutions because no good idea gets lost in the noise of people talking over each other.

03

Body Language

What is your posture saying when your mouth is closed?

What Goes Wrong

Crossed Arms, Slouched Back

Sitting back with arms crossed, checking your phone, or turning your body away from the group signals disinterest even if you have not said a single negative word. The room reads it immediately.

How to Build It

Open Up Your Posture

Sit forward slightly. Keep your arms uncrossed. Nod when you agree. Face the person speaking. These small physical adjustments signal that you are engaged and make others feel comfortable contributing.

Why It Matters

Your Body Votes Too

Studies show the majority of communication is nonverbal. If your body language contradicts your words, people will trust what your body is doing. Positive body language keeps energy high in the group.

04

Giving Feedback

Can you respond to ideas without shutting people down?

What Goes Wrong

Tearing Ideas Apart

When someone shares an idea and gets immediately dismissed or mocked, they will not share again. Harsh or careless feedback kills creativity and makes the group a place people want to leave.

How to Build It

Acknowledge, Then Add

Start by recognizing what is useful about an idea before raising concerns. Say what works first. Then offer your thoughts as a build, not an attack. Keep it about the idea, never the person.

Why It Matters

Good Feedback Grows the Group

Constructive feedback is how ideas go from good to great. When people feel safe receiving and giving feedback, the group keeps improving instead of stagnating around the first idea someone threw out.

05

Being Present

Is your mind actually in the room right now?

What Goes Wrong

Physically Here, Mentally Gone

You can be sitting at the table and completely checked out at the same time. Scrolling, daydreaming, or mentally working on something else means you are contributing nothing and taking up a seat.

How to Build It

Commit to the Meeting Duration

Before the meeting starts, close other tabs, put the phone face down, and make a deliberate choice to be fully in the conversation. Give the group the same focus you would want from them.

Why It Matters

Presence Is a Form of Respect

When everyone is truly present, the meeting takes half the time and produces twice the results. Full presence is the foundation that all the other skills are built on. Without it, nothing else works.

After Training

Same Group, Different Energy

Good Skit Video
  • Members are making natural eye contact with whoever is speaking. The room feels connected and attentive.
  • People let each other finish before responding. Ideas land fully before the next person adds to them.
  • Everyone is leaning in, nodding, and facing the speaker. The body language says "we are all in this together."
  • Feedback is constructive. Members build on each other's ideas instead of tearing them down.
  • Phones are away and attention is undivided. The group moves quickly because everyone is fully present.
